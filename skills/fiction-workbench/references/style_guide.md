# Fiction Anti-Slop Style Guide

Use this as the default style discipline for `fiction-workbench` when a project does not provide stricter local guidance. If `bible/style-guide.md` exists, let the project file override these defaults.

Apply these rules conservatively. One odd sentence is usually just texture. A cluster of the same move is the problem.

## Good Patterns

- Keep narration grounded in point of view, scene pressure, and specific physical detail.
- Let implication carry subtext. Prefer gesture, interruption, sensory noticing, and selective omission over explanation.
- Vary sentence length by dramatic need, not by formula.
- Use plain verbs where possible. Let image, action, and choice create intensity.
- Let paragraph breaks track turns in attention, pressure, setting, or emotional position.
- Keep dialogue slightly more patterned than reality but less polished than an essay.
- Preserve deliberate voice quirks already established in the chapter or project bible.

## Sentence Rhythm

- Aim for a flexible average around 15 to 25 words in ordinary narration.
- Mix short, medium, and long sentences, but avoid visible patterning.
- Do not use three short sentences in a row for emphasis unless the source voice clearly depends on that staccato rhythm.
- Avoid repeating the same opener across adjacent sentences unless it is an intentional voice feature.
- Avoid stacked sentence fragments that create trailer-copy drama.
- Prefer one precise sentence over a staged sequence such as `Not X. Not Y. Just Z.`
- Let long sentences earn their length through perception, motion, or thought, not abstract explanation.

## Paragraph Rhythm

- Use paragraph breaks for shifts in action, speaker, perception, decision, time, or emotional pressure.
- Avoid single-line paragraphs used only to manufacture importance.
- Avoid several same-length paragraphs in a row when the scene should breathe differently.
- Do not make every paragraph end on a reveal, reversal, or portentous beat.
- Keep exposition braided into conflict, action, memory, or immediate need.
- Compress repeated interiority. If the character has already understood the feeling, show what it makes them do next.

## Bad Patterns And Banned Rhetorical Patterns

- Inflated abstraction: `truth`, `silence`, `darkness`, `memory`, `grief`, `desire`, or `the weight of it` used as generic emotional fog.
- Negative reveal structure: `It was not fear. It was something worse.`
- Staged realization: `Only then did she realize...`, `What he did not know was...`
- Omniscient tease: narration hinting at future knowledge the current point of view cannot hold.
- Rule-of-three lyricism: three adjectives, three images, or three clauses arranged for canned grandeur.
- Theatrical contrast: `not because X, but because Y` repeated as a default mode of insight.
- Explainer narration: the prose tells the reader what the scene means instead of letting the scene exert pressure.
- Generic cinematic beats: hearts hammering, breaths catching, blood running cold, silence stretching, time slowing, rooms holding their breath.
- Over-clean dialogue: characters explain exactly what they feel, want, fear, or symbolize.

## Revision Priorities

1. Preserve canon, point of view, tense, and story intent.
2. Remove repeated rhetorical scaffolds and forbidden patterns.
3. Replace abstract emotional labels with concrete action, perception, or implication.
4. Vary sentence and paragraph rhythm where repetition creates artificial emphasis.
5. Cut exposition, summary, or interior repetition that does not change the scene.
